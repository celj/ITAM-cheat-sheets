4 Economics 4

4.1 General equilibrium

Pure exchange economies

Initial assumptions

  • There are \(m\) consumers such that \(\mathcal{I} = \{1, \dots, m\}\).
  • There are \(n\) goods such that \(\mathcal{L}= \{1, \dots, n\}\).
  • A utility function \(u_i : \mathbb{R}^\mathcal{L}\to \mathbb{R}\) represents the preferences of the \(i\)th consumer.
  • Each consumer can consume goods in \(\mathrm{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^\mathcal{L}_+\).
  • Each consumer has an initial endowment of \(\mathrm{w}_i \in \mathbb{R}^\mathcal{L}_+\).
  • The ordered pair \((u_i, \mathrm{w}_i)\) describes each consumer.
  • The utility functions represent neoclassical preferences.

Proposition 4.1 If \(\mathrm{x} \succ_i \mathrm{y}\), then \(u_i(\mathrm{x}) > u_i(\mathrm{y})\).

Definition 4.1 (Exchange economy) A pure exchange economy is \[ \mathcal{E} = \left\langle \mathcal{I}, (u_i, \mathrm{w}_i)_{i\in\mathcal{I}} \right\rangle, \] where \(\mathcal{I}\) is the set of agents; \(u_i\) and \(\mathrm{w}_i\) are the utility function and initial endowment of the \(i\)th consumer, respectively.

Definition 4.2 (Total endowment) \[ \Omega = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} \mathrm{w}_i. \]

Definition 4.3 (Resource allocation) The resource allocation is denoted by \(X = (\mathrm{x}_1, \mathrm{x}_2, \dots, \mathrm{x}_m)\), where \(\mathrm{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^\mathcal{L}_+\).

Definition 4.4 (Feasible allocation) The feasible allocation \(\mathcal{F}\) of an economy \(\mathcal{E}\) is defined by \[ \mathcal{F}= \left\{ X = (\mathrm{x}_1, \mathrm{x}_2, \dots, \mathrm{x}_m)\ :\ \mathrm{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^\mathcal{L}_i,\ \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} \mathrm{x}_i = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} \mathrm{w}_i \right\}. \]

Definition 4.5 (Pareto efficiency) Let \(\mathcal{E}\) be an exchange economy. A feasible allocation of resources \(X\) is said to be Pareto efficient if and only if there is no other feasible allocation \(\hat{X}\) such that, for every agent in \(\mathcal{I}\), \(u_i(\hat{\mathrm{x}}_i) \geq u_i(\mathrm{x}_i)\) and, for at least one agent \(j\), \(u_j(\hat{\mathrm{x}}_j) > u_j(\mathrm{x}_j)\).

Definition 4.6 (Pareto dominance) Let \(X\) and \(\hat{X}\) be two feasible allocations. We say that \(\hat{X}\) Pareto dominates \(X\) if and only if \[ u_i(\hat{x}_{i,1}, \dots, \hat{x}_{i,n}) \geq u_i(x_{i,1}, \dots, x_{i,n}), \quad \forall i \in \mathcal{I}, \] and there is at least one consumer \(j\) such that \[ u_j(\hat{x}_{j,1}, \dots, \hat{x}_{j,n}) > u_i(x_{j,1}, \dots, x_{j,n}). \]

Definition 4.7 (Contract curve) The set of all Pareto allocations is known as the contract curve.

General case

\[\begin{align*} \max_{\{ \mathrm{x}_1,\ \dots\ ,\ \mathrm{x}_m \}} \quad & u_1(x_{1,1}, \dots, x_{1,n}) \\ \text{subject to} \quad & u_2(x_{2,1}, \dots, x_{2,n}) \geq \bar{u}_2, \\ & \quad \vdots \\ & u_m(x_{m,1}, \dots, x_{m,n}) \geq \bar{u}_m; \\ & \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} x_{i,1} \leq w_1, \\ & \quad \vdots \\ & \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} x_{i,n} \leq w_n. \end{align*}\]

Theorem 4.1 Let all utility functions be strictly increasing and quasiconcave, and \(\hat{X}\) be a feasible interior allocation. Then \(\hat{X}\) is Pareto efficient if and only if \(\hat{X}\) exhausts all resources and, for all pairs of goods \((\ell, \ell')\), \[ \text{MRS}(\ell, \ell')(\hat{x}_{1,1}, \dots, \hat{x}_{1,n}) = \cdots = \text{MRS}(\ell, \ell')(\hat{x}_{m,1}, \dots, \hat{x}_{m,n}). \]

Competitive equilibrium

Initial assumptions

  • There is a market for each good.
  • Every agent can access the market without any cost.
  • There is a single price for each good.
  • All consumers know the price.
  • Each consumer can sell their initial endowment in the market and use the income to buy goods and services.
  • Consumers seek to maximize their utility given their budget restriction, independently of what everyone else is doing.
  • There is no centralized mechanism.
  • People may not know others’ preferences or endowments.
  • There is perfect competition (namely, everyone is a price-taker).
  • Prices are the only source of information for agents.

Definition 4.8 (Competitive equilibrium) An ordered pair of an allocation and a price vector, \(\left( X^*, \mathrm{p} = (p_1, \dots, p_n) \right)\), is called a competitive equilibrium if the following conditions hold:

  1. \(\forall i \in \mathcal{I}\), \(\mathrm{x}_i^* = (x_{i,1}^*, \dots, x_{i,n}^*)\) solves the following maximization problem: \[\begin{align*} \max_{\{ \mathrm{x}_i \}} \quad & u_i(\mathrm{x}_i) \\ \text{subject to} \quad & wh + \langle \mathrm{p}, \mathrm{x}_i \rangle \leq \langle \mathrm{p}, \mathrm{w}_i \rangle = \sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{L}} p_{\ell} w_{i,\ell}. \end{align*}\]
  2. Markets clear, i.e. \(\displaystyle \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} x_{i,\ell}^* = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} w_{i,\ell}\), \(\forall \ell \in \mathcal{L}\).

Proposition 4.2 Given at least one consumer with strictly monotonic preferences. Then, if \((X^*, \mathrm{p})\) is a competitive equilibrium, \(p_1, p_2, \dots, p_n >0\).

Proposition 4.3 Given at least one consumer with weakly monotonic preferences. Then, if \((X^*, \mathrm{p})\) is a competitive equilibrium, for at least one \(\ell\), \(p_\ell > 0\).

Proposition 4.4 Let \((X^*, \mathrm{p})\) be a competitive equilibrium. Then, \((X^*, c\mathrm{p})\) is also a competitive equilibrium, \(\forall c \in \mathbb{R}_+\).

Theorem 4.2 (Walras's Law) If the consumer \(i\) has weakly monotonic preferences and \(\hat{\mathrm{x}}_i \in \mathrm{x}_i^*(\mathrm{p})\), then \[ \langle \mathrm{p}, \hat{\mathrm{x}}_i \rangle = \sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{L}} p_{\ell} \hat{x}_{i,\ell} = \sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{L}} p_{\ell} w_{i,\ell} = \langle \mathrm{p}, \mathrm{w}_i \rangle. \]

Corollary 4.1 (Walras's Law) Given weakly monotonic utility functions and \(\mathrm{p} = (p_1, \dots, p_n)\) such that \(p_\ell > 0\). If any \((X^*, \mathrm{p})\) in which maximization condition holds, \(\forall i \in \mathcal{I}\), and markets clear \(\forall \ell = 1, 2, \dots, n - 1\); then, the market clearing condition holds for commodity \(n\) as well.
